Overview â€” How Tidal Power Works

  1. Predictable resource: Tides follow astronomical cycles â†’ forecastable output.
  2. Two approaches: (a) Range (barrage/lagoon) uses water level head; (b) Stream uses current velocity.
  3. Turbomachinery: Axial/cross-flow rotors convert kinetic/head energy to shaft power.
  4. Grid interface: Power conditioned and stepped up for distribution.
  5. Operational planning: Dispatch scheduled around ecology, navigation & flood control.

Source of Energy

  1. Gravitational forcing: Earthâ€“Moonâ€“Sun interactions cause sea-level & current cycles.
  2. Site potential: Range: tidal amplitude & impoundment; Stream: current speed & channel cross-section.
  3. Capacity factor: Typically moderate; improved via multiple turbine strings/energy storage.

Conversion Process (Step-by-Step)

  1. Siting & civil works: Barrage/lagoon gates (range) or monopiles/tri-pods (stream).
  2. Hydraulics: Water level head or channel flow directed across rotors.
  3. Electro-mechanical: Rotor â†’ gearbox (or direct-drive) â†’ generator.
  4. Power conditioning: Rectifiers/inverters, harmonic filtering, and protection relays.
  5. Grid intertie: Transformer step-up, switchgear, SCADA/telemetry.

Output / Utilization

  1. Electricity to coastal loads or export to main grid.
  2. Hybridization with batteries to shape delivery within the tidal cycle.
  3. Community benefits: Harbor lighting, cold-storage, EV charging, desal add-ons.

Real-world Relevance

  1. Reliability: Highly predictable generation windows.
  2. Longevity: Marine civil assets with multi-decade lifetimes.
  3. Planning: Fish passage, sediment transport, migratory species, acoustic footprint.
  4. Low visual impact: Subsea turbines largely invisible once deployed.
